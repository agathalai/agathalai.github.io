Glossary

    4
  • 4-acid digestion
    Uses a combination of hydrochloric acid (HCl), nitric acid (HNO3), hydrofluoric acid (HF) and perchloric acid (HClO4). HF dissolves silicate minerals, this digestion is often referred to as 'near-total digestions'. It will still not dissolve phases like chromite, barite, monazite, sphene, xenotime, rutile, etc. and can lose some volatile elements (e.g. B, As, Pb, Ge, Sb).
    • A
  • ABA
    see "acid-base accounting"
  • acid-base accounting
    A screening procedure whereby the balance between the acid-neutralizing potential (NP) and the acid-generating potential (AP) of rock samples is determined.
  • acid-generating potential
    The maximum potential for a rock sample to generate acidity. It is commonly expressed in units of kg CaCO3/t given the amount of carbonate needed to neutralize the amount of acid capable of being produced.
  • acid rock drainage
    The acidic water that is generated when sulphide minerals are exposed to air and water producing sulphuric acid.
  • AP
    see "acid-generating potential"
  • aqua regia
    A mixture of nitric acid (HNO3) and hydrochloric acid (HCl) in a molar ratio of 1:3. This digestion will not fully dissolve silicate minerals. Aqua regia digests are suitable for dissolution of carbonate, oxide and certain sulfides, and provides a semi-quantitative dissolution of precious and base metals.
  • ARD
    see "acid rock drainage"
    • C
  • carbonate buffering
    The most important buffer system in natural surface waters and wastewater treatment, consisting of a carbon dioxide, water, carbonic acid, bicarbonate, and carbonate ion equilibrium that resists to changes in the water's pH.
  • carbonate speciation
    The various carbonate species that are possible to find in a mineral.
  • conductivity
    The ability of a solution to transmit an electrical current. A high conductivity indicates a solution with a high charged ion content. Often expressed in µS/cm.
  • crustal abundance
    The average abundance of an element in rock types that are part of the Earth’s crust.
    • E
  • elemental deportment
    Describes how a specific element is distributed in the mineral phases of a sample.
  • elemental speciation
    The chemical form in which an element is present in a mineral or in a solution.
    • G
  • GCM
    see "geochemical conceptual model"
  • geochemical conceptual model
    The hypotheses about mine waste characteristics and reactivity which are subsequently evaluated through observations, sampling, testwork and interpretation.
    • H
  • humidity cells
    Weathering (i.e. kinetic) tests used to measure rates of oxidation, neutralization and element leaching of a sample. Humidity cell tests are designed to mimic weathering at the laboratory scale in a controlled fashion.
    • I
  • ICP-MS
    see "inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry"
  • ICP-MS elemental scan
    Provides a concentration determination for up to 50 elements in a sample. This analysis is intended as a survey technique, providing a means to identify the elemental composition of a sample.
  • inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry
    An analytical technique used for elemental determinations. An ICP-MS combines a high-temperature ICP (Inductively Coupled Plasma) source with a mass spectrometer (MS). The ICP source converts the atoms of the elements in the sample to ions. These ions are then separated and detected by the mass spectrometer.
    • M
  • metal leaching
    The process of solid material containing metals and other elements being dissolved into an aqueous solution.
  • ML
    see "metal leaching"
  • ML/ARD
    metal leaching & acid rock drainage
  • multi-element scan
    Provides a concentration determination for up to 50 elements in a sample. This analysis is intended as a survey technique, providing a means to identify the elemental composition of a sample.
    • N
  • neutralizing potential
    The neutralizing potential of a rock sample is the general term to express its capability to neutralize applied acidity. It is commonly expressed in units of kg CaCO3/t.
  • NP
    see "neutralizing potential"
    • P
  • PAG
    see "potentially ARD generating"
  • paste pH
    The pH of the solution created by mixing the pulverized sample with deionized water at various solid/water ratios. It is best used for weathered samples to evaluate if a sample has produced ARD or not.
  • potentially ARD generating
    A rock sample that is likely to generate acidity over a certain time period once it is exposed to atmospheric oxygen and water.
    • Q
  • QEMSCAN
    see "quantitative evaluation of minerals by scanning electron microscopy"
  • quantitative evaluation of minerals by scanning electron microscopy
    An integrated automated mineralogy and petrography system providing mineralogical information on polished samples. The system identifies minerals based on chemistry and a reference database. It is also possible to estimate mineral deportment of certain elements and mineral liberation.
    • S
  • SFE
    see "shake flask extraction"
  • shake flask extraction
    A static bench scale laboratory test used to assess the dissolution of soluble mineral phases from a sample in deionized water (e.g. near-neutral pH solution). The MEND method consists in a solid/liquid ratio of 1/3 and of < 6.35 mm sample size distribution.
  • sulphate sulphur
    The sulphur attributed to the sulphate content of a sample. It can be determined by turbidimetry in an aqueous solution.
  • sulphide sulphur
    The sulphur attributed to the sulphide content of a sample.
    • T
  • TC
    see "total carbon"
  • TIC
    see "total inorganic carbon"
  • total carbon
    All the carbon present in the sample, including both inorganic and organic carbon.
  • total inorganic carbon
    The carbon present in the sample as carbonate, bicarbonate, and dissolved carbon dioxide (CO2).
  • total sulphur
    All the sulphur present in the sample.
    • X
  • x-ray diffraction
    A mineralogical identification technique based on mineral structure. This technique is often coupled with the Rietveld refinement method to provide a semi-quantitative estimate of mineral abundance in a sample.
  • x-ray fluorescence
    An analytical technique used to determine the chemical composition of a sample. XRF analyzers determine the chemistry of a sample by measuring the fluorescent (or secondary) X-ray emitted from a sample when it is excited by a primary X-ray source.
  • XRD
    see "x-ray diffraction"
  • XRF
    see "x-ray fluorescence"

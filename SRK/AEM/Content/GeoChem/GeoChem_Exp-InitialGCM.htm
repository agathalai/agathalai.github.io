Geochemical Conceptual Model

The development of a GCMgeochemical conceptual model is a way to capture the key considerations for how mine waste will react once it is placed in a storage facility. The GCMgeochemical conceptual model effectively represents hypotheses about mine waste characteristics and reactivity which are subsequently evaluated through observations, sampling, test work and interpretation. For example, placing rock with sulphide minerals on the surface versus underwater can result in very different outcomes with regards to water chemistry. Ultimately the GCMgeochemical conceptual model will be used to develop the geochemical management plan for the respective site.

Through compilation and interpretation of the recommended information in this section, an initial GCMgeochemical conceptual model can be developed to identify geochemical risks. An example of a GCMgeochemical conceptual model for a greenstone gold project during early exploration could be something like the following:

  • Mineralization in the deposit area is unoxidized with sulphide minerals (pyrite and chalcopyrite) presents in drill core.
  • Pyrite and chalcopyrite will oxidize in the atmosphere. This will apply particularly to any long-term rock storage facilities, water unsaturated tailings, exposed pit walls, TSF dams, etc.
  • Sulphide oxidation will release acidity, sulphate and copper. If the pH remains neutral, copper concentrations should also remain low given the strong solubility control on this mineral.
  • Carbonates are extensive in the deposit, but siderite is noted in drill core, with lesser calcite. Because siderite is a poor buffering carbonate, there may be a higher potential for ARDacid rock drainage.
  • Gold appears to be closely associated with sulphides and, therefore, tailings may be preferentially PAGpotentially ARD generating when compared to other potential mine rocks.
  • A nearby mine in the same geological setting also leaches zinc at neutral pH, which may indicate that zinc is present in pyrite given that sphalerite was not noted during drill core logging.
  • Multi-element scans confirm above average concentrations of zinc and copper and no other elements were noted to be above typical concentrations in basalt rock types—after the crustal abundanceThe average abundance of an element in rock types that are part of the Earth’s crust. comparison with Price (1997).

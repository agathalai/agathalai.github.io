Approach

There are many aspects to environmental assessment of a mining project from geotechnical to community relations. These studies, such as environmental geochemistry or ML/ARDmetal leaching & acid rock drainage characterization of mine waste (i.e., noneconomical or waste rock, tailings and other processing residues, borrow materials, pit walls, etc.), are needed for several reasons including the following:

Mine waste will be left on site indefinitely and poses one of the most significant long-term liabilities of a mining operation. How it will react over the long term is critical to understand.

It is (or should be) a key input to environmental engineering and mine design and is an iterative process.

Water treatment requirements will be directly impacted by mine waste management practices.

Mineral processing and tailings management can be impacted by tailings reactivity or present opportunities to reduce long-term geochemical risk.

The main objective of an environmental geochemistry characterization program is to understand how mine waste will impact the receiving environment once the waste is placed in a new storage facility. Typically, this means evaluating the interactions with the atmosphere, and therefore weathering and leaching of minerals by water; but this can also include water submergence after oxidation (e.g., flooding of a pit with backfilled waste rock or tailings) or sub-aqueous disposal of tailings. This is important because if the mine waste produces acidity, some elements will become orders of magnitude more soluble (e.g., copper, lead, zinc), whereas other elements are more soluble at neutral pH and in the absence of ARDacid rock drainage (e.g., arsenic, selenium, molybdenum), and can lead to problematic water quality at neutral pH.

Two examples that illustrate the relationship between pH and element solubility are provided in Figure 2. The graphs plot data from waste rock seepage at operating mines in British Columbia (Day and Rees 2006) and show that while copper becomes more solubles as pH decreases (typical of most metals), other elements like molybdenum, arsenic, selenium are most soluble at neutral pH.

There are many sources of information on geochemical characterization available for those users of this guide who would like additional technical information. The two main sources include the following:

  • The Global Acid Rock DrainageThe acidic water that is generated when sulphide minerals are exposed to air and water producing sulphuric acid. (GARD) guide (INAP 2010). This is a wiki-style online document that can be found at http://www.gardguide.com/
  • The Mine Environment Neutral Drainage (MEND) Prediction Manual for Drainage Chemistry from Sulphidic Geologic Materials (MEND (1991) and MEND (2009)). These are reference documents prepared under the direction of MEND but with various specialist contributors. The tests proposed in this guide are all referenced in these manuals.

The characterization approach of this guide is to identify key geochemical risks so that Agnico Eagle can internally determine the best management approach. Testing has not been developed to support permitting applications and, as a result, many tests required for permitting and regulatory requirements are not included in this guide (e.g., kinetic testing in humidity cellsWeathering (i.e. kinetic) tests used to measure rates of oxidation, neutralization and element leaching of a sample. Humidity cell tests are designed to mimic weathering at the laboratory scale in a controlled fashion., Quebec Directive 019 tests, etc.). However, it is expected that any of the data from an internal program could be used to support permitting activities.

Testing requirements increase with each project stage, much like geological and economic development studies of an ore deposit. For early-stage projects, the focus is on the deposit type, mineralization and geochemical information typically collected with exploration activities. As a project develops (or is more advanced), more detailed study is required to understand oxidation, leaching and possibly sequestration processes of the mine waste.

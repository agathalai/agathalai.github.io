Geological Settings

The geographical location and geological province of a deposit will impact its leaching characteristic and will need to be considered as part of testing. As recommended in Initial Exploration Stage, discussions with project geologists should take place to utilize existing knowledge base on a deposit and ensure testing needs are incorporated at the beginning of a project.

Testing outlined in the previous sections should be applied to all deposits and recommendations below are for regional considerations. Some preliminary recommendations are provided in Table 5.

The recommendations in this section are not exhaustive and are expected to develop through revisions to this guide, but more importantly have to be guided by the geochemical conceptual modelThe hypotheses about mine waste characteristics and reactivity which are subsequently evaluated through observations, sampling, testwork and interpretation. developed for the site in the first stage of characterization. For example, if during the review of the region or an analogue site, a unique geochemical characteristic is noted (e.g., fluorite), then testing for the same geochemical characteristic should be included.

The rationale and guidance for tests described in Table 5 are described in the following subsections: Shake Flask Extraction (SFE) Tests, Carbonate Mineralogy, Sulphur Mineralogy, and Arsenic Mineralogy.

Shake Flask Extraction (SFE) Tests

These tests assess the readily soluble components of the mine rock or processing waste (i.e., tailings and heap leach materials). When the results from the test are corrected for water contact ratios and particle size, water chemistry can often be predicted and therefore provide an input to potential water quality for the site.

Guidance is provided by MEND (2009) and typically includes 100 g of minus 6.35 mm material mixed with 300 mL of deionized water (1:3 ratio) and shaken for 24 hours (although 250 g and 750 mL is typical at some labs). The leachate is then submitted for water chemistry analysis for all major anions, cations, and multi-elements. The test is relatively dilute compared to mine sites and needs to be adjusted. Conversely, loading rates are often overestimated as complete water contact and small particle size often results in release rates higher than observed in the field.

Sampling frequency would be the same as mineralogy – approximately 50% of the samples selected during stage II (Scoping Study Stage).

A template and calculation example are provided in Geochemical Data Interpretation Template, Stage 2, SFE_tests.

Carbonate Mineralogy

These are the same tests as outlined in the Scoping Study Stage section. The modified Sobek NPneutralizing potential titration test is best described in MEND (1991) and uses room temperature acid for the titration.

Sulphur Mineralogy

Mineralogy characterization and sulphur analyses need to consider the various sulphur forms. Sulphate minerals may be neutral leaching (e.g., gypsum) or leach acidity (e.g., jarosite).

Arsenic Mineralogy

The mineral association of arsenic determines its leaching characteristics. For example, arsenic can be present in the sulphide mineral pyrite or as an arsenide in arsenopyrite. With pyrite, arsenic leaching is of less of a concern given the relatively high proportion of iron that can adsorb arsenic and remove it from solution. Arsenic from arsenides, on the other hand, has minimal iron and arsenic is often more soluble, especially at neutral pH.

This same approach can usually be taken for many other typical elements of interest (e.g., zinc, lead, molybdenum, etc.).

Sampling frequency would initially be the same as general mineralogy samples (Scoping Study Stage).

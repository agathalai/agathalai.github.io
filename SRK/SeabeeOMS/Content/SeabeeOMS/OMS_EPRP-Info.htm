Failure modes and Effects Analysis (FMEA)

An FMEA was carried out during completion of the Triangle Lake TMF expansion detailed design (SRK 2018b). The FMEA identified controls to manage high-consequence risks and potential emergency situations associated with a TMF. It was then used to facilitate development of an in-depth bow-tie risk assessment focused on the overarching failure mechanisms for the TMFs that would result in an emergency response. The bow-tie assessment is included in Appendix C.

Indundation Study

A high-level inundation study was also completed in accordance with CDA Guidelines to assess the impacts to the downstream receptors as a resultant of dam/dike failure at either the East Lake or Triangle Lake TMFs. The study considered an unrealistic worst-case scenario consisting of a full release of the tailings contained within the East Lake and/or Triangle Lake TMF to either Laonil or Pine lakes.

A first study evaluated whether the tailings would be fully contained beneath a water cover in these lakes. Considering the bathymetric data for each lake and the total deposited volume of tailings in each facility, the study concluded that the respective downstream lakes would hold the total volume of tailings contained in the upstream TMF.

This study was followed by a second analysis to determine if the tailings released into the adjacent lakes would settle within the lakes or remain in suspension and potentially migrate further downstream (SRK 2019c). By calculating the volume of tailings released during a dam break, this study led to the following conclusions:

  • Potential failure of Triangle Lake TMF toward Pine Lake would result in 1.3 Mm3 of tailings and 760,000 m3 of supernatant released.
  • Potential failure of East Lake TMF toward Laonil Lake would result in 210,000 m3 of tailings and 120,000 m3 of supernatant released.
  • The receptive lake (Laonil and/or Pine) would be impacted immediately under such a failure; however, all tailings particles will settle out of suspension into the lakes.
  • As a precautionary measure, turbidity curtains are to be readily available at site so that they can be installed in the event of a downstream failure into either Pine Lake or Laonil Lake.
  • Post-impact studies would have to be completed immediately following any uncontrolled release of tailings and/or supernatant water.
  • Remedial efforts required would be based on results of the post-impact studies.

These results provided guidance toward developing the reactive controls and associated tasks listed in this ERP if a full or partial release of tailings and supernatant were to occur.

A concise summary of the inundation study is provided in Figures 9, 10, and 11.

MAC Guidelines for the Crisis Management Team (CMT)

Complete details of the crisis management plan and CMT, including roles and responsibilities, are provided in the Mining Association of Canada’s “Crisis Management and Communications Planning Reference Guide” (MAC 2018).

A brief description of each CCMT member’s responsibility as outlined in MAC (2018) is provided below:

  • Chair. Responsible for overseeing the development and implementation of the crisis management plan.
  • Crisis Coordinator. Responsible for the development, review, training, and distribution of the crisis management plan to all members.
  • Company Spokesperson. Responsible for communication with the media, local communities, and other key stakeholders.
  • Communications Coordinator. Assists the Crisis Coordinator with development of the crisis management plan, specifically in the sense of resources available to monitor and handle the company’s response to media and stakeholders.
  • Human resources coordinator. Responsible for the creation of confidential personnel records, including contact information and proof-of-life questions.
  • Legal counsel. Responsible for identifying, in advance, legal resources, requirements, and constraints related to potential crises.
  • Administrative Assistant. Responsible for providing administrative support to the CMT.
